What to Look for in an Outdoor Grill Station

1. Grill Opening Width and Compatibility (30–42 inches)
The single most critical spec is the grill opening width. Standard built-in grills are 30, 36, or 42 inches wide; your station frame must accommodate your existing grill or the one you plan to buy. Summerset's 32-inch opening fits most common sizes, but Artisan Outdoors stations reach 42 inches (meaning you have options later). Measure your grill's exterior width now—this is non-negotiable. A station that's too small forces you to use aftermarket spacers (poor heat distribution) or buy a new grill entirely (expensive). Count on spending an extra $400–$800 if you need to replace your grill to fit the frame.

2. Counter Depth, Heat Shielding, and Work Surface (20–24 inches minimum)
Counter depth determines whether you have usable prep space and how close the grill sits to your appliances. Lynx Professional stations offer 24 inches (luxury standard); most cart-style units hover at 18–20 inches (cramped for plating). More important: heat shielding. High-quality stations (Summerset, Artisan, Lynx) include insulated rear panels or ceramic fiber backing to protect adjacent structures from radiant heat. Low-cost carts (under $400) often have thin metal only—if your station is near siding or a deck, you'll need 3–4 inches of clearance behind, reducing usable deck space. Real-world owner data: homes with unshielded budget carts in tight spaces report warping or discoloration of walls within 2–3 seasons.

3. Stainless Steel Grade and Rust Resistance (304 vs. 430)
Not all stainless is equal. Grade 304 stainless (used in Summerset, Artisan, and Lynx) resists saltwater and humid-climate rust for 10+ years with minimal care. Grade 430 stainless (cheaper models) corrodes visibly within 18–24 months in coastal or high-moisture zones. Check the spec sheet—if it doesn't state the grade, assume 430. Powder-coated steel (GrillPro, Broil King) is durable but requires annual touch-ups in wet climates. Test: if you live within 5 miles of ocean or in the Pacific Northwest, spend the extra $300–$400 for 304 stainless or a sealed powder-coat system. Budget alone won't protect you here.

4. What to Avoid: Common Budget Traps and Misleading Marketing
Particle-board or MDF countertops (sold in discount models under $350) absorb water and delaminate within one season—avoid entirely. "Stainless appearance" paint on steel frames will chip and rust within months; demand a verified material spec. Undersized storage compartments (under 20 cubic feet) become unusable quickly; owners report they can't fit a propane tank, tools, and utensils simultaneously. Vague warranty terms ("lifetime warranty on frame" but only 90 days on hardware) are red flags—Summerset's 5-year all-parts coverage is the standard to match. Finally, avoid stations without adjustable feet; uneven patios will cause doors to sag and shelves to tilt within a season.

5. Budget Calibration: When to Spend More vs. When Budget Works
Under $600 (GrillPro, Char-Broil): Buy here if you're renting, trying outdoor cooking for the first time, or live in a dry climate (Southwest, Midwest). These carts are portable and easy to upgrade later. Don't buy here if you live in a humid zone or plan to keep the station beyond 5 years—rust will end you. $700–$1,200 (Summerset, Broil King): Sweet spot. Modular design, 304 stainless options, proper heat shielding, and 5-year warranties. If you own your home and cook outdoors more than 20 times a year, spend here. The durability premium (20-year lifespan vs. 5-year) pays off within three seasons in wet climates. $1,300+ (Artisan, Lynx Professional): Premium tier justifies cost only if you're adding a second grill, smoker, or refrigerator into the station, or your patio is on public display (homeowner's pride, not function). Granite countertops and modular burner integration add $400–$600 in material costs but zero functional cooking benefit. Buy here if you're doing a full kitchen remodel or live in a climate that demands maximum corrosion resistance (coastal properties).
