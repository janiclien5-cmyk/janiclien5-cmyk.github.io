What to Look For

What Makes a Good Heat Pump: The 3 Specs That Actually Matter

1. SEER2 Rating (Cooling Efficiency) — The Single Most Important Spec: SEER2 measures cooling efficiency under real-world conditions (not lab conditions). A unit rated 15 SEER2 uses roughly 33% less electricity than a 10 SEER2 model. The Carrier Infinity at 21 SEER2 cuts cooling costs by ~40% versus the Goodman at 16 SEER2. Budget models start at 14–16 SEER2; mid-range at 17–19 SEER2; premium at 20+ SEER2. Over 15 years, the difference between 15 and 21 SEER2 amounts to $3,000–$5,000 in electricity savings, often justifying a $400–$600 upfront premium.

2. HSPF2 (Heating Efficiency) — Critical If You Live North of Zone 5: HSPF2 measures heating output relative to energy input—higher is better. A 10 HSPF2 unit wastes energy in cold climates; 12+ HSPF2 is excellent and maintains heating even when outdoor temps drop to 5°F. The Daikin Fit achieves 13 HSPF2, making it best-in-class for northern climates where heating is 60% of annual load. The Goodman's 8.5 HSPF2 means it will trigger expensive electric backup heat more often if you live in Maine, Minnesota, or Vermont. Check your climate zone at energystar.gov before prioritizing SEER2 alone.

3. Compressor Type (Inverter-Driven vs. Fixed-Capacity) — Why Newer Models Cost Less to Run: Modern units use inverter-driven compressors that modulate output (like a car's cruise control), running at 30% power on mild days rather than cycling full-on/full-off. This cuts short-cycling losses and noise. The Carrier uses inverter technology; older budget models still sold online do not. Expect 15–25% lower electricity use and 3–5 dB quieter operation from inverter units—worth the $200–$400 extra upfront.

What to Avoid: Common Traps and Misleading Terms

Don't confuse legacy SEER (pre-2023 standard) with SEER2. A 16 SEER model under old rules is roughly 12–13 SEER2 under current standards. Any online listing showing only "SEER 16" without the "2" is either outdated or from an older unit. Also avoid "AHRI-matched" systems sold as generic bundles—cheap indoor units paired with mid-range outdoor units often underperform because mismatched coil sizes waste 10–15% efficiency. Reputable installers always use matched pairs (e.g., Carrier with Carrier). Finally, don't fall for WiFi as a primary buying factor; smart controls add $300–$600 and rarely save more than 5% on usage because most homeowners set them-and-forget-them. Prioritize efficiency rating, not app-enabled scheduling.
