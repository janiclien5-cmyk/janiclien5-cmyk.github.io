The Most Important Spec: Peak Brightness (Measured in Nits)

Peak brightness is the single most critical number for 85-inch TVs because it determines whether HDR content (movies, sports, video games) actually looks like HDR or like a washed-out standard image. The human eye perceives brightness logarithmically—jumping from 500 nits to 1,000 nits feels roughly twice as bright—so don't overlook this number. The Hisense U8N delivers 3,000+ nits peak brightness, the Samsung QN90D reaches 2,500+ nits, and OLED models like the LG C4 max out around 200 nits peak (the trade-off for perfect blacks). For bright living rooms or sports viewing, 2,000+ nits is the minimum threshold; below that, HDR impact flattens in daylight.

The Second-Most Important Spec: Local Dimming Zones

Local dimming zones allow the TV to brighten and darken different parts of the screen independently, creating contrast without blooming (bright halos around dark objects). The Hisense U8N leads here with 55,000+ zones (nearly 1 zone per pixel), the TCL QM8 brings 6,144 zones, and the Sony X95L offers 1,152 zones. Entry-level Mini LED TVs like the Hisense U7N have fewer than 1,000 zones, which means the backlight can't respond quickly to fast-moving objects—you'll see black crush in night scenes or loss of detail during fast cuts. For movies and gaming, 1,000+ zones is a meaningful upgrade; 5,000+ zones is where you stop noticing blooming artifacts entirely.

What to Avoid: Common Budget Traps and Misleading Claims

First, avoid any 85-inch TV under $1,200 that advertises "60Hz upscaled to 120Hz"—this is a marketing trick. Native 120Hz support (meaning the TV panel actually refreshes 120 times per second) matters only if you game, watch sports, or stream fast action content; it eliminates the judder that upscaling cannot fix. Second, be skeptical of "peak brightness" claims that measure a 10% window rather than full-screen white—the Hisense U8N's 3,000 nits is measured across a full white screen, but some competitors claim 4,000+ nits in a tiny window that bears no relation to real-world viewing. Third, check for HDMI 2.1 port count: gaming at 4K 120Hz or using the TV as a monitor requires at least one HDMI 2.1 port (minimum 48Gbps bandwidth). The Hisense U8N and Sony X95L both include HDMI 2.1; many budget models do not. Finally, skip TVs with "smart TV" platforms you've never heard of—stick to Roku, Google TV, or Samsung Tizen, which receive regular updates and don't go obsolete in three years.

How to Calibrate Your Budget: When Spending More Actually Pays Off

The jump from $649 (Hisense U7N) to $899 (TCL QM8) gets you Mini LED contrast and Dolby Vision support—worth it if you watch HDR movies or play games. The jump from $899 to $1,299 (Hisense U8N) adds 55,000 local dimming zones and peak brightness above 3,000 nits—this buys brightness in bright rooms and eliminates blooming, justifying the price if your living room has large windows or south-facing light. The jump from $1,299 to $1,499 (LG C4 OLED, Samsung QN90D) is a marginal gain: OLED offers perfect blacks for dark movie watching, but loses brightness outdoors; Samsung adds anti-glare coating for bright rooms. For most buyers, the sweet spot is $1,100–$1,300 (Hisense U8N or Sony X95L). If your room is dim or you prioritize movie watching, spend the extra $200 for OLED; if you have bright daytime light or game frequently, save $200 with the TCL QM8 and invest in a good soundbar instead.
