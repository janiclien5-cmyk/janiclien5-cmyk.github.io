What to Look For

1. Water Column Rating (Minimum Spec You Can't Ignore)
The single most important metric is the tent floor's water column rating, measured in millimeters (mm) of water pressure the fabric can withstand. Any tent floor rated below 1,200mm will leak during moderate summer rain within the first two seasons; 1,500mm is the practical minimum for reliable three-season camping. For reference, the REI Co-op Camp Hut 4 features a 1,500mm polyester floor, the Coleman Sundome reaches 1,000mm (acceptable for summer-only use), and premium options like the Nemo Dragonfly push to 2,000mm+ for four-season reliability. This rating matters more than wall thickness or brand name—we've seen expensive boutique tents fail at 800mm ratings, while budget tents with 1,500mm floors outlast them by years.

2. Packed Weight vs. Livable Space (Your Real Trade-Off)
Weight capacity splits into two contradictory demands: ultralight backpacking tents (2–3 lbs total, under 20 cubic feet interior) versus car-camping tents (8–12 lbs, 40+ cubic feet). The Kelty Salida 1 weighs 3.5 lbs but offers surprisingly usable interior space (19.5 cubic feet), while the Coleman Sundome 2-Person checks in at 5.5 lbs with 32 cubic feet. Most summer campers don't need ultralight—they need "not annoying to carry," which is 4–7 lbs for a two-person tent. Above 10 lbs, you're either sleeping three people or sacrificing weight for durability you'll actually appreciate after your fifth trip.

3. Avoid These Budget Traps and Marketing Lies
Don't buy any tent marketed as "waterproof" (only water-resistant tents exist—waterproof fabric blocks breathability and creates condensation that pools on your sleeping bag). Avoid single-wall construction below $75; those tents promise weight savings but deliver humidity issues that ruin summer trips in 60%+ conditions. Skip no-name brands on Amazon with ratings from fewer than 200 reviews (verification becomes impossible, and you'll face boot-camp-quality control). Beware "seasonal" tents rated for "3-4 seasons" that actually mean "summer and occasional spring," not true year-round use. Finally, ultra-cheap floor protectors (ground tarps under $15) actually accelerate wear by trapping moisture—the REI Co-op and Nemo products include adequate floor protection, while budget Coleman models assume you'll buy a proper footprint.

4. Budget Calibration: When More Money Solves Real Problems
Under $75, expect 1–2 seasons of reliable use before fabric degradation. $75–$200 (the sweet spot) delivers 4–6 years of regular use with honest durability and weight compromises. $200–$400 adds premium materials (DCF fabrics, lighter pole systems) and better weatherproofing (2,000mm+ floor ratings). Above $400, you're paying for ultralight engineering or four-season capability—genuinely useful only if you camp in extreme conditions regularly. For the typical summer camper (May–September, established campgrounds or car-accessible sites), the $120–$200 range includes everything you need. Spending $400+ makes sense only if you backpack 20+ nights per year in unpredictable weather.

What should I avoid when buying camping gear?

Avoid any tent with a floor water column rating below 1,200mm—those fail within 2-3 seasons when humidity exceeds 60%. Does weight matter in summer camping gear?

Weight matters if you backpack (carrying your tent matters) but becomes irrelevant if you car-camp (your tent rides in a vehicle).
